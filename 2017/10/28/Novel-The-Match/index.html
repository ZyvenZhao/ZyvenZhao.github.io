The Match

The little man came up to me as I was to enter the telephone box, and asked me whether I had a match. “I’m sorry,” I said. “I don’t smoke, so I haven’t any. You had better ask someone else.”

He looked disappointed, hesitated, and then turned away. I watched him walk slowly down the street before I picked up the telephone directory to look up the number I meant to dial.

I am not used to a public call box so, at my first attempt to get through, the warning pips had stopped before I realized I had to insert a coin.

When I was at last able to speak, I was told that the person whom I urgently wanted to give a message to had just that minute gone out.

Swearing slightly under my breath, I emerged from the box and came face to face with the little man, who was looking as pathetic as a astray dog. As he raised his hat again, I could see he was quite bald. A thin line, resembling a duelling scar, crossed one cheek. He spoke nervously.

“Excuse my troubling you again,” He said. “May I walk along with you a little way? I must confide someone. I need help desperately.”

“I’m really sorry, but I don’t know you,” I said shortly, “and I’m in a hurry.”

“Just for a while, I beg you, sir.” He looked like he was about to cry. Empty and nearly all the shops were closed, the street looked like deserted as the darkness fell. I got a glimpse at my watch, it was five past seven. Seeing that the person I intended to call might be out for dinner and wouldn’t be back soon, walking for a while was not a bad idea.

“All right,” I accepted the little man’s invitation, “but I have to get back soon.” On hearing this, he looked rather pleased and repeated his thanks.

We walked along the street, quietly and slowly. I felt a little cold and tightened the collar and then put my hand into the pocket of my wind-breaker. But he seemed no fear for the cold notwithstanding the cold wind. I noticed that there was a hole in his left sock. Maybe he was accustomed to the cold rather than not afraid of it.

“I was born in the Great Depression,” He broke the silence and started to tell me about his story, “and my father got unemployed and became a gambler. Soon we got bankruptcy and we had to beg for a living.” I could feel his voice was trembling but I could get nothing to him for solace.

“What can I do for you?” I asked. But he didn’t reply.

Crossed the road and turned right, we walked into a dead end. Suddenly, he stopped. “I’m hungry,” He turned around, “and I need some money.”

Being astonished, I stopped,too. The world quietened as I could hear the wind blowing across my ears. With the dim light, I could see his eyes turned into sinister from pitiful. Seeing that I was cheated and embroiled myself in a robbery. The trivial things began to flash in my mind. The image of my daughter running into me for a hug, my wife’s complain about the dog urinating on the carpet again, my unfinished working documents heaping on the desk… I stepped backward unconsciously. He got instantly on the alert. “You’d better freeze, sir.” He nodded at his pocket and indicated the knife in it. “It’ll be fine if you do as what I say.”

“But I didn’t take much money. Had it not been for the malfunction of the phone, I wouldn’t come out in this terrible night.” I tried to calm myself down in case that he would do something crazy.

“Then give all what you have taken.” He appeared to be rather disappointed.

I searched my pocket and took almost everything out and gave all them to him. Except from several coins I was about to make the pone call and the cheap wrist watch , I didn’t take anything useful to him. “Is that all?” He doubted if I was telling a lie.

“It seems that you’ve robbed the wrong people, I’m afraid.” I gave him a wry smile.

“Then I wanna check your pockets by myself.” He got somewhat impatient and stepped forward.

“Trust me, you won’t like it.” I told him with sincerity.

“Stop talking rubbish. Be quick!” His nearly got out of control and his eyes filled with greed. But his face got ghastly pale as soon as I took out my gun.

He appeared to be rather submissive when I handcuffed him and then he was sent to the police station.

Again I looked at my watch, thirty minutes passed.As the phone call got through, the familiar voice sounded.

…

“I was intended to tell you to be cautious, but you went out then.”

“But how did you know that he was a robber?” He asked me with curiosity.

“Honestly speaking, I didn’t know. But there’s a shop on the corner that hasn’t closed yet. And…”

“What’s wrong?”

“His knife was so sharp that his pocket was poked a hole.”
